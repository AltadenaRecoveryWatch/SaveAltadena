Eaton Fire Recovery · Community Action

Stop speculators from taking Altadena

A fair recovery — not a fire sale.

What's happening

After the Eaton Fire, outside investors are buying burned lots from survivors — often elderly, underinsured families — for far less than they're worth, then using a state law, SB 1123, to pack up to 10 units onto a single family's lot with no public hearing. Many sellers may not be getting fair value for their land.

Altadena has long been a diverse, family-rooted community. Unchecked speculation strips generational wealth and displaces people who've lived here for decades. This isn't about blocking housing — it's about protecting survivors.

The three things we're asking for

1

Demand emergency action — Supervisor Barger must press the Governor for an executive order

The Palisades got an emergency order — but it was tied to a fire-hazard map that covers the Palisades and leaves most of Altadena out. We didn't get a protection written for us; we got a hand-me-down that doesn't fit. As our representative and SB 1090's sponsor, Supervisor Barger has the standing to fix that — she must press the Governor for an order protecting the whole Altadena disaster area now (covering both SB 9 and SB 1123). SB 1090 doesn't take effect until 2027, and survivors can't wait.

2

Pass and strengthen SB 1090

Make the protection run from the fire date, Jan 7, 2025, through Jan 7, 2030, and cover every project without an issued building permit as of the earliest possible date — so speculators can't race to file first. SB 1090 is still pending; even if it passes it may only protect going forward, which is why the emergency order can't wait.

3

Carve Altadena out of SB 1116

We're not trying to block SB 1116 statewide — only to exempt our disaster area so Altadena can recover before these density laws are expanded.

What would SB 1116 actually do?

SB 1116 (Sen. Caballero) strengthens California's Starter Home Revitalization Act. Its stated goal is to build more for-sale "starter homes." For Altadena, the concern is that it makes burned-lot subdivision easier — and harder to scale down locally. It would:

  • Shrink minimum lot sizes — new parcels in multifamily zones could be as small as 480–960 sq ft.
  • Require the subdivision rules to be read "liberally, to maximize the number of units."
  • Measure building height in feet, not stories, and bar cities from requiring certain setbacks — removing local tools to scale projects down.
  • Exclude stairs and enclosed bike parking from size limits, allowing more bulk.
  • Void private deed restrictions (CC&Rs) that block this density — except in HOA / common-interest communities.
  • Apply to charter cities as a "matter of statewide concern."
  • Add some reporting and oversight requirements.

Our ask isn't to stop SB 1116 statewide — only to carve out the Eaton Fire disaster area while Altadena rebuilds.
