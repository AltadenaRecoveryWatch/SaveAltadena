Who we are

Altadena Recovery Watch is a community effort to protect Altadena's recovery from the Eaton Fire.

We are a group of fire survivors, residents, property owners, community businesses, and more that are concerned about the future of Altadena. We want to ensure that Altadena's recovery results in a resilient community that has some resemblance of our roots and also welcomes new community members to enjoy what makes Altadena special.

Groups we work with include but are not limited to: Altadena Heritage · Save Altadena · Beautiful Altadena.

Where We Stand

Our position on SB 1090 — tap any point to expand.

Parity, nothing more.
The Palisades got this exact protection by executive order (EO N-32-25). Altadena — which lost 45% more, nearly 9,000 homes, businesses, and structures — asks only for the same.
Disaster capitalism is not a fair housing solution.
Out-of-town developers can carve a displaced family's lot into ten by-right units before that family even returns. A survivor may rebuild just 10% larger; a developer next door gets ten times the units. SB 1123 makes it easier to speculate than to come home — the Legislature shouldn't let the market profit off a community that lost everything.
SB 1090 protects the SHRA's original purpose.
The Starter Home Revitalization Act was built for scattered urban infill on abandoned lots — not fire-cleared ones. SB 1090 safeguards that intent; it doesn't repeal the law.
Without SB 1090, SB 1123 pushes a statewide crisis onto one vulnerable community.
It dumps California's housing shortage onto a single, not-yet-recovered disaster zone — historically marginalized, home to renters who lost two-thirds of their rent-stabilized housing, and still without rebuilt roads, sewer, water, or grid to carry more density.
It will brand the SHRA the “Altadena Fire Sale Act.”
A program meant to model responsible infill instead becomes the law that let investors carve up a burn scar — discrediting the very policy.
Altadena's own framework already delivers high density in our prime corridors.
We welcome real, mixed-use density where it belongs — in our commercial corridors. And even with SB 1090, every single-family lot already allows four homes (the house plus a local ADU, a state ADU, and a junior ADU) under our Community Standards District and the County ADU ordinance. Let our own planning framework guide the rebuild, not state laws written for a different reality.
